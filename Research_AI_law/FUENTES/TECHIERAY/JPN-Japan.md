[5 December 2025] Government to ease data consent rules for AI development:
It is reported that Japan plans to ease legal restrictions on the use of personal information to accelerate the development of AI while penalizing violations. According to inside sources, an undisclosed draft bill to Japan's Personal Information Protection Law calls for amendments including enabling third parties to obtain individuals' sensitive information -- such as medical history and criminal records -- without consent for the purpose of statistical data generation and academic research (and provided it is processed into statistical form, rather than shared in a way that could identify individuals). The draft bill also calls for introducing administrative monetary fines to prevent the use of data for profit. This covers cases of serious human rights violations by businesses that unlawfully acquire and sell personal information of more than a thousand people. In such cases, they will be charged an amount equivalent to the profit gained through the misconduct. However, violations resulting from accidental data leaks or inadequate security management would not be subject to these penalties. To protect children’s rights, the revision would mandate that consent for collecting data from individuals under age 16 must be obtained from a legal guardian rather than the child. The dual approach--regulatory relaxation for AI development alongside stricter penalties for abuse--reflects a compromise between industry demands and public concerns over privacy and human rights. The Personal Information Protection Law is reviewed every three years, and the Personal Information Protection Commission has been discussing revisions since 2023. The government is seeking to submit the amendment to the Diet at the earliest possible date.
