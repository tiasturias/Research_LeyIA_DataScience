China (People's Republic of China)
Regulation Status
China does not have a single comprehensive AI law. Instead, it regulates AI through a patchwork of sector-specific regulations, administrative measures, and technical standards targeting key applications such as recommendation algorithms, generative AI, deep synthesis, synthetic content labelling, and data governance. There is ongoing debate and no clear timeline for a unified national AI law, with legislative efforts delayed due to disagreements over timing and scope.
China’s regulatory approach is highly adaptive and iterative, with frequent updates, new draft measures, and evolving technical standards reflecting rapid technological change and political priorities.
Regulatory enforcement is centralized and highly coordinated, with the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) and other ministries playing leading roles in both rulemaking and compliance oversight, including mandatory registration, security assessments, and government-led model reviews.
China’s AI governance emphasizes national security, social stability, and alignment with state interests, with explicit requirements for content control, data localization, and risk management, as well as strong extraterritorial reach for foreign providers operating in China.
China is actively shaping global AI governance through bilateral, multilateral, and UN initiatives, promoting principles of sovereignty, inclusivity, and non-discrimination, while also seeking to influence international standards and frameworks.

The guidelines identify five primary risk categories associated with AI, including weakening human control and discrimination, and propose six core principles: people-centred design, safety and controllability, fairness and justice, transparency, collaborative governance, and inclusive benefit-sharing. [7 March 2025] Changshu People's Court rules in a case that an AI-generated image is eligible for copyright protection:
The Changshu People’s Court has announced that it had ruled in a case that images generated with AI are eligible for copyright protection. In this case, the plaintiff (Lin) generated an image of a half heart in a city waterfront using Midjourney and further used Photoshop to edit the image. An unnamed Changsha real estate company then used the image in a WeChat posting and further built a three-dimensional installation based on the image at one of its developments. The Court found that (1) based on its review of the Midjourney user agreement, the assets and rights of the pictures produced by the user using the Midjourney service belong to the user; (2) Lin’s modification of the prompts and the modification of the picture through the image processing software reflected his unique selection and arrangement, and the image generated by this was original and belonged to the works protected by the Copyright Law; (3) the real estate company violated Lin's copyright by disseminating the picture on the Internet without the permission of the copyright owner; (4) the copyright enjoyed by Lin were limited to the picture, and the manufacturing of the three-dimensional installation was only based on the image; and (5) the real estate company’s design and construction of the corresponding installation did not constitute an infringement of Lin’s copyright. The Changshu People’s Court ordered the defendants to issue a public apology and pay 10,000 yuan (approximately $1,380) in damages. This is believed to be the second case regarding AI-generated images with the Beijing Internet Court ruled similarly in late 2023. In its announcement on Weixin, the deputy chief judge of the Changshu People’s Court, Hu Yue, stated that the case underlines that AI-generated images can be afforded copyright protections so long as human creativity went into the making of a work.

The article argues that the key AI competition is unfolding among different strategic camps inside China rather than primarily between China and the US, with three distinct models of “AI leadership” emerging.
