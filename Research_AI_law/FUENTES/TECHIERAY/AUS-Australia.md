Regulation Status
❗️ This tracker only tracks developments at the Commonwealth (federal) level.
No AI-specific regulation yet. But based on Australia's National AI Plan (2025), the government will steer clear of introducing any standalone AI specific legislation, such as the previously proposed mandatory guardrails for high-risk AI, and instead build on existing legal and regulatory frameworks, in addition to other voluntary frameworks and guidance (such as the Voluntary AI Standards). Various law reforms in other specific areas (e.g. privacy, online safety, consumer law) will also incidentally address AI.
AI ethics framework available (federal level). A comprehensive AI assurance framework available at federal, state and territory levels.

[2 December 2025] Australia National AI Plan released:
The Australian Government has launched the National AI Plan to pursue three overarching goals, each with a clear set of actions to deliver them. The first goal, “Capture the opportunity”, focuses on growing an AI-enabled economy by building AI-ready digital and physical infrastructure (such as data centres and compute), backing domestic AI research and innovation, and attracting global investment so Australian firms can develop and export high‑value AI products and services. The second goal, “Spread the benefits”, aims to ensure all regions, industries and communities share in AI-driven productivity gains by scaling AI adoption in businesses (especially SMEs), investing in workforce skills and training so workers can use and shape AI, and embedding AI across government services through initiatives like the APS AI Plan and GovAI platform to improve service delivery. The third goal, “Keep Australians safe”, seeks to manage risks and maintain public trust by using and updating existing laws rather than creating a standalone AI Act, establishing institutions such as an AI Safety Institute, strengthening oversight of automated decision-making, and promoting responsible, risk‑based practices and international cooperation so AI systems are fair, accountable and aligned with Australian values.

The Australian government has announced it will establish an AI Safety Institute to assess risks from emerging artificial intelligence systems and safeguard Australians from potential harms. The AISI's work will include: (1) helping government keep pace with rapid developments in AI technologies, dynamically addressing emerging risks and harms; (2) enhancing understanding of technical developments in advanced AI and potential impacts; (3) serving as a central hub to share insights and support coordinated government action; (4) giving guidance on AI opportunity, risk and safety for businesses, government and the public through established channels including the National AI Centre (NAIC); and (5) supporting Australia’s commitments under international AI safety agreements. [5 September 2024] Australian Government launches consultation on proposed mandatory guardrails for high risk AI:
The Australian Government has launched a consultation on a new proposed set of mandatory guardrails for AI in high risk settings. The discussion paper sets out the (1) proposed definition of high-risk AI; (2) 10 proposed mandatory guardrails; and (3) three regulatory options to mandate these guardrails. The consultation closes on 4 October 2024. The proposed mandatory guardrails are: (i) Establish, implement and publish an accountability process including governance, internal capability and a strategy for regulatory compliance; (ii) Establish and implement a risk management process to identify and mitigate risks; (iii) Protect AI systems, and implement data governance measures to manage data quality and provenance; (iv) Test AI models and systems to evaluate model performance and monitor the system once deployed; (v) Enable human control or intervention in an AI system to achieve meaningful human oversight; (vi) Inform end-users regarding AI-enabled decisions, interactions with AI and AI-generated content; (vii) Establish processes for people impacted by AI systems to challenge use or outcomes; (viii) Be transparent with other organisations across the AI supply chain about data, models and systems to help them effectively address risks; (ix) Keep and maintain records to allow third parties to assess compliance with guardrails; and (x) Undertake conformity assessments to demonstrate and certify compliance with the guardrail. In terms of regulatory approaches, the three proposed options are: (a) Adopting the guardrails within existing regulatory frameworks as needed; (b) Introducing new framework legislation to adapt existing regulatory frameworks across the economy; or (c) Introducing a new cross-economy AI-specific law (for example, an Australian AI Act).

[3 October 2025] Australia Treasury releases final report on AI and the Australian Consumer Law:
The Australian Treasury's Review of Artificial Intelligence and the Australian Consumer Law (ACL) confirms that existing consumer protections effectively cover AI products and services, ensuring safety and fairness comparable to traditional goods and services. The report made six findings: (1) the principles-based protections of the ACL are generally well-suited to address consumer law risks associated with AI-enabled goods and services; (2) uncertainty in distinguishing between goods and services, including those with AI, creates a barrier to ACL application, suggesting clarity could be improved by targeted amendments to the definition of 'goods' and updated regulatory guidance; (3) the current remedy and liability provisions remain appropriate, but clarity on obligations across the AI supply chain could be enhanced through amendments to the definition of 'manufacturer'; (4) existing manufacturer defences for defective goods are broadly appropriate, though technical amendments may be needed to ensure they operate correctly for software-enabled goods, especially where the manufacturer retains post-supply control; (5) the nature of AI-enabled products does not require specific changes to the powers of the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC), though regular review of these powers should continue; and (6) the ACL provides Australian consumers of AI-enabled products with similar, and sometimes enhanced, protections compared to consumers in the EU, UK, and Singapore.

Proposal for mandatory guardrails for AI in high-risk settings (2024):
This paper sets out the (1) proposed definition of high-risk AI; (2) 10 proposed mandatory guardrails; and (3) three regulatory options to mandate these guardrails. Specific data risks include low-quality or biased datasets, data poisoning, and exposure of training data. [14 November 2024] OECD releases paper on assessing potential future AI risks, benefits and policy imperatives, including the top 10 policy priorities:
The OECD has released a report which reviews research and expert perspectives on potential future AI benefits, risks and policy actions. It features contributions from members of the OECD Expert Group on AI Futures ("Expert Group"), which is jointly supported by the OECD AI and Emerging Digital Technologies division (AIEDT) and Strategic Foresight Unit (SFU). Among other findings, the report identified 66 potential policy approaches to obtain AI benefits and mitigate risks, with the top 10 policy priorities being: (1) establish clearer rules, including on liability, for AI harms to remove uncertainties and promote adoption; (2) consider approaches to restrict or prevent certain “red line” AI uses; (3) require or promote the disclosure of key information about some types of AI systems; (4) ensure risk management procedures are followed throughout the lifecycle of AI systems that may pose a high risk; (5) mitigate competitive race dynamics in AI development and deployment that could limit fair competition and result in harms, including through international co-operation; (6) invest in research on AI safety and trustworthiness approaches, including AI alignment, capability evaluations, interpretability, explainability and transparency; (7) facilitate educational, retraining and reskilling opportunities to help address labour market disruptions and the growing need for AI skills; (8) empower stakeholders and society to help build trust and reinforce democracy; (9) mitigate excessive power concentration; and (10) take targeted actions to advance specific future AI benefits.

The UNESCO and OECD, with contributions from the G7, have developed a toolkit for policymakers and public sector leaders to translate principles for safe, secure, and trustworthy AI into actionable policies. Key messages of the toolkit are: (1) establish clear strategic objectives and action plans in line with expected benefits; (2) include the voices of users in shaping strategies and implementation; (3) overcome siloed structures in government for effective governance; (4) establish robust frameworks for the responsible use of AI; (5) improve scalability and replicability of successful AI initiatives; (6) enable a more systematic use of AI in and by the public sector; and (7) adopt an incremental and experimental approach to the deployment and use of AI in and by the public sector.

[FRAMEWORK] OECD AI Principles (2019):
The OECD AI Principles promote use of AI that is innovative and trustworthy and that respects human rights and democratic values. Adopted in May 2019 and updated in May 2024, they set standards for AI that are practical and flexible enough to stand the test of time. Values-based principles include (1) promoting inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being, (2) respecting human rights and democratic values, (3) ensuring transparency and explainability, (4) robustness, security and safety, and (5) fostering accountability and responsibility. Recommendations for policymakers include (1) investing in AI research and development, (2) fostering an inclusive AI-enabling ecosystem, (3) sharping an enabling interoperable governance and policy environment for AI, (4) building human capacity and preparing for labor market transition, and (5) international cooperation for trustworthy AI.
